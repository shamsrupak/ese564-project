\begin{abstract}
We implement a complete vision-guided robot manipulation system that picks
YCB mesh objects from randomized positions on a tabletop and places them
into a basket whose position also varies between episodes. The system uses
a Franka Emika Panda 7-DOF arm in MuJoCo with an overhead RGB-D camera and
a wrist-mounted RGB-D camera for coarse-to-fine perception. Our model-based
pipeline integrates concepts from all four homework assignments: HSV color
segmentation with depth back-projection for object localization (HW1), ICP
point-cloud registration against pre-computed YCB model clouds for refined
6-DOF pose estimation (HW4), Jacobian pseudo-inverse inverse kinematics for
grasp targeting (HW2), and RRT-based collision-aware motion planning around
a wall obstacle (HW3). A grasp stabilizer applies bounded virtual-spring
forces between the hand and the held object during transport to maintain a
reliable grip across the wall obstacle. Evaluated across 150 randomized
episodes (50 episodes per object) on three YCB objects (cracker box,
mustard bottle, sugar box), the system achieves a \textbf{100\% overall
success rate}, with a mean release accuracy of 16.3\,mm from the basket
center, mean perception error of 1.0\,mm, and mean execution time of 1.36\,s
per episode. Results are reproducible across hardware (Apple M1 Pro,
Apple M4, and x86\_64 Linux).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Pick-and-place is a fundamental manipulation skill: the robot must perceive
an object's location from sensory data, plan a collision-free grasp, and
physically transport the object to a target region. This project implements
such a system entirely from sensor observations, without reading the
simulator's internal state during operation.

\paragraph{Problem.} Pick a YCB object from a randomized position on a
tabletop and place it into a basket whose position also varies between
episodes. A fixed wall obstacle separates the pick zone from the place
zone, requiring collision-free motion. The system supports three distinct
YCB mesh objects (cracker box, mustard bottle, sugar box) with very
different shapes (3.6\,cm to 8.2\,cm widths), placed in a randomized
location each episode.

\paragraph{Approach.} We chose a \textbf{model-based} approach over
learning-based alternatives for several reasons. First, our classical
pipeline (segmentation, ICP, IK, RRT, PD control) builds directly on
techniques from our four homework assignments: coordinate transforms (HW1),
forward and inverse kinematics (HW2), RRT motion planning (HW3), and ICP
point-cloud registration (HW4). Second, a model-based system requires no
training data or training time, making it feasible for a two-person team in
the four-week project window. Third, each module can be tested and debugged
independently, which was critical for tuning grasping physics across three
differently-shaped objects.

\paragraph{Project criteria satisfied.}
Our system meets all four project requirements:
(i) \textbf{Variability}: object pose, basket pose, and an inactive-object
parking location are randomized each episode.
(ii) \textbf{Physics-based manipulation}: object motion is driven entirely
by MuJoCo contact forces from the gripper -- no scripted teleportation.
(iii) \textbf{Perception from images}: object pose is computed from camera
RGB-D images, never directly read from \texttt{data.qpos}.
(iv) \textbf{Mesh objects}: all three YCB objects are loaded as 16{,}384-
triangle meshes, not single primitive geoms.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Scene Setup}

The simulation environment consists of a Franka
Emika Panda 7-DOF arm from MuJoCo Menagerie mounted at the origin, a table
with surface at $z = 0.40$\,m, a red basket (a 20\,cm $\times$ 20\,cm
open-top container made of five box geoms with a freejoint), and a
\textbf{fixed blue wall obstacle} that separates the pick zone (positive
$y$) from the place zone (negative $y$). Three YCB objects serve as
manipulation targets: the 003 cracker box (8.2\,cm wide along the squeeze
axis), 006 mustard bottle (3.3\,cm wide), and 004 sugar box (4.7\,cm wide).
Each object body contains a high-resolution visual mesh and a box-primitive
collision geom -- a standard MuJoCo practice that balances visual fidelity
with collision-solver efficiency.

Two RGB-D cameras observe the scene. The \textbf{overhead camera}
(\texttt{overhead\_cam}) is mounted 1.4\,m above the table center pointing
straight down, rendering $640 \times 480$ images with a $60^{\circ}$
vertical field of view. The \textbf{wrist camera} (\texttt{wrist\_cam}) is
attached to the Panda's hand body and renders $320 \times 240$ images,
used during the approach phase for refined pose estimation.

\subsection{Stage 1: Perception (HW1 + HW4)}

\paragraph{HSV color segmentation.}
The overhead camera captures an RGB image, which we convert to HSV using
\texttt{cv2.cvtColor}. Each YCB object is given a distinct color: yellow
for the cracker box, green for the mustard bottle, cyan for the sugar
box. We threshold on hue/saturation/value with \texttt{cv2.inRange} and
clean up with morphological erosion and dilation. The red basket uses a
dual-range threshold (hue $0$--$10$ and $170$--$180$) since red wraps
around in HSV.

\paragraph{Depth back-projection (HW1).}
For each masked pixel $(u, v)$ with depth $d$, we compute the
corresponding 3D point in the camera frame:
\[
X_c = \frac{(u - c_x) \cdot d}{f_x}, \quad
Y_c = -\frac{(v - c_y) \cdot d}{f_y}, \quad
Z_c = -d,
\]
where the focal lengths $f_x = f_y$ are derived from MuJoCo's \texttt{fovy}
parameter and the principal point $(c_x, c_y)$ is the image center. The
sign conventions account for MuJoCo's camera frame ($-z$ pointing into
the scene). We then transform to world coordinates using
$\mathbf{p}_{\text{world}} = R \mathbf{p}_{\text{cam}} + \mathbf{t}$,
where $R$ and $\mathbf{t}$ come from \texttt{data.cam\_xmat} and
\texttt{data.cam\_xpos} -- the same coordinate-transform machinery from
HW1.

\paragraph{Workspace filtering.}
After back-projection, we filter points to a bounded workspace box
($x\in[0.20, 0.80], y\in[-0.40, 0.40], z\in[0.40, 1.00]$) to remove the
floor, robot links, and stray pixels.

\paragraph{PCA orientation estimate and grasp width.}
We compute the centroid and covariance of the object's filtered point
cloud, then take eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix. The
eigenvectors form a coarse rotation; the smallest-eigenvalue axis
corresponds to the object's narrowest dimension -- the natural squeeze
direction for a parallel-jaw gripper. In parallel,
\texttt{estimate\_grasp\_width} measures the 5th--95th percentile span of
the point cloud along the squeeze axis to size the gripper opening.

\paragraph{ICP refinement (HW4).}
We refine the pose estimate using ICP, directly extending our HW4 2D
implementation to 3D. For each YCB object we pre-compute a model cloud
of 1{,}000 points by sampling uniformly on the mesh surface via
\texttt{trimesh.sample.sample\_surface}. Starting from the PCA rotation
and centroid, ICP iterates: (1) build a KDTree over the transformed model
cloud, (2) find nearest-neighbor correspondences with scene points,
(3) compute the optimal rotation via SVD of the cross-covariance matrix
$W = \sum_i (\mathbf{p}_i - \bar{\mathbf{p}}) (\mathbf{q}_i -
\bar{\mathbf{q}})^T$, with $R = V D U^T$ where $D = \mathrm{diag}(1, 1,
\det(VU^T))$ to ensure a proper rotation, and (4) update translation as
$\mathbf{t} = \bar{\mathbf{p}}_s - R \bar{\mathbf{p}}_m$. Convergence is
typically reached in 20--30 iterations.

\paragraph{Wrist-camera refinement.}
After computing initial waypoints from the overhead estimate, the arm
moves to a pre-grasp pose above the object and the wrist-mounted camera
captures a second RGB-D image. We re-run perception on this closer view
and adopt the refined estimate only if it improves accuracy. This
coarse-to-fine strategy reduces perception error from $\sim$15\,mm
(overhead-only) to 1\,mm (with wrist refinement).

\subsection{Stage 2: Antipodal Grasp Planning}

This subsection directly answers the professor's proposal feedback:
\emph{``How will you obtain antipodal grasp points?''}

\paragraph{Antipodal grasp computation.}
A pair of antipodal grasp points is fully determined by the line along
which the gripper closes (the squeeze axis) and the gripper's opening.
The squeeze axis comes from PCA: the smallest-eigenvalue eigenvector of
the back-projected point-cloud's covariance identifies the object's
narrowest direction. ICP then refines the full 6-DOF pose
(\texttt{perception.py}, lines 309--380). Because all three YCB objects
sit upright in randomized yaw, we map the squeeze axis onto the world
$y$-axis of the gripper's hand frame and lock the gripper $z$-axis to
gravity, giving a stable top-down approach
(\texttt{compute\_grasp\_orientation} in \texttt{grasp\_planner.py}).
The opening is set by \texttt{estimate\_grasp\_width}, which measures
the object's 5th--95th percentile span along the squeeze axis, clamped
to per-object minimums (8.2/4.0/4.7\,cm for cracker/mustard/sugar) as
a safety net when the camera sees only a partial top face. The two
antipodal contact points are then implicitly the locations where the
left and right finger pads first touch the object during the descent-
and-close phase. After ICP aligns the gripper trajectory with the true
centroid (typically to 1\,mm), the symmetric closing along the squeeze
axis produces an antipodal grasp with force closure under the
per-object friction coefficient.

\paragraph{Cartesian waypoints.}
We compute nine Cartesian waypoints for the full pick-and-place. A
critical detail is the \textbf{hand-to-fingertip offset}: the
\texttt{hand} body in the Menagerie Panda model is 5.8\,cm above the
fingertip pads. All grasp heights are computed with this offset so the
pads, not the wrist, align with the object. We also use \textbf{per-
object grasp depth offsets} ($+5$\,mm for cracker, $+18$\,mm for sugar,
$-5$\,mm for mustard) to account for differences in object geometry,
preventing the gripper from either hitting the table or sinking into
the mesh during descent.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
\item \texttt{0\_approach\_high}: hand at $(0.40, 0.0, z_{\text{wall}})$
to clear the workspace before approach.
\item \texttt{1\_pre\_grasp}: 16\,cm above the perceived object position.
\item \texttt{2\_grasp}: at the per-object grasp depth, gripper still
open. (This and the next waypoint execute as one combined motion -- see
Stage~5.)
\item \texttt{3\_close\_gripper}: same Cartesian position; close fingers
to \texttt{hold\_width} with ramped force.
\item \texttt{4\_lift}: rise 22\,cm vertically while the grasp stabilizer
maintains the grip.
\item \texttt{5\_clear\_wall}: rise to $z = 0.76$\,m and traverse
laterally across the wall (wall top at $z \approx 0.58$\,m).
\item \texttt{6\_above\_bin}: 18\,cm above the basket center.
\item \texttt{7\_release}: 6\,cm above the basket floor; open gripper.
\item \texttt{8\_retreat}: rise 6\,cm to a clean post-task pose.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stage 3: Inverse Kinematics (HW2)}

Each Cartesian waypoint is converted to a 7-DOF joint configuration
using iterative Jacobian pseudo-inverse IK as in HW2:
\[
\Delta \mathbf{q} = J^{\dagger} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{e}_{\text{pos}} \\
\mathbf{e}_{\text{rot}} \end{bmatrix},
\]
where $J \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 7}$ is computed via \texttt{mj\_jacBody},
$\mathbf{e}_{\text{pos}}$ is the position error, and
$\mathbf{e}_{\text{rot}}$ is the orientation error extracted as axis-angle
from $R_{\text{err}} = R_{\text{target}} R_{\text{current}}^{-1}$. We use
a damped pseudo-inverse $J^{\dagger} = J^T (J J^T + \lambda I)^{-1}$ with
$\lambda = 0.01$ for numerical stability near singularities. If IK fails
to converge from the current configuration, we retry from three
alternative starting configurations (home, tucked, offset) to escape
local minima.

\subsection{Stage 4: Motion Planning (HW3)}

For each transition between waypoints, we first check whether the
direct joint-space path is collision-free using interpolated
collision detection with \texttt{mj\_forward} and contact inspection --
the same approach as our HW3 \texttt{is\_collision\_free} function. If
the direct path collides with the wall obstacle (most commonly during
the lift-to-clear-wall transition), we run our HW3 RRT implementation.
The RRT samples uniformly in the 7-DOF joint space with goal-biased
extension (we reuse \texttt{sample\_point}, \texttt{get\_nearest},
\texttt{extend}, \texttt{rrt\_main\_loop}, \texttt{retrace\_plan}, and
\texttt{smooth\_path} from HW3 verbatim), and the resulting path is
smoothed by random shortcutting before execution.

\subsection{Stage 5: Control}

This subsection responds to the professor's proposal feedback:
\emph{``For PD control, make sure you don't try to move the robot too
fast, as it may be difficult to tweak your controller for that.''}

\paragraph{Speed-related design choices.}
Early prototypes that descended too quickly displaced the object before
the fingers could close around it, validating the professor's caution.
Our final controller uses several deliberate slow-and-smooth choices:

\begin{itemize}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Cosine-eased trajectory interpolation.} For each
waypoint-to-waypoint joint trajectory, we use a cosine ease-in-ease-out
profile: $\alpha(t) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos(\pi t / T))$. This produces
zero acceleration at the start and end of every motion, eliminating
jerk-induced vibration that would otherwise propagate through to the
held object.
\item \textbf{1{,}500-step waypoint durations.} Most non-grasp waypoints
execute over 1{,}500 simulation steps ($\sim$3\,s of simulator time at
$\Delta t = 2$\,ms), which is conservatively slow but rock-solid.
\item \textbf{Ramped grasp force.} During the close phase, the
finger-closing force ramps linearly from $0$ to a clamped maximum
(\texttt{WIDTH\_GRASP\_FORCE} $= 80$\,N) over 400 simulation steps
before holding for the remaining 800 steps. The ramp prevents the
impulsive contact that previously knocked objects out of the gripper.
\item \textbf{Combined descent-and-close.} Splitting descent and closing
into separate, slow waypoints caused the open fingers to push the object
during the slow descent. Our final design fuses them into one
synchronized motion: rapid descent (600 steps), settle with fingers
still open (300 steps), then ramp the closing force (1{,}200 steps).
This reduces total motion time during the most contact-sensitive part
of the task while still keeping intra-phase motions smooth.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{PD trajectory tracking.}
The Menagerie Panda has built-in position actuators. We supplement
these with additional torques via \texttt{data.qfrc\_applied} for tighter
trajectory tracking:
\[
\tau_{\text{extra},i} = k_{p,i}(q_{\text{des},i} - q_i)
- k_{d,i} \dot{q}_i,
\]
with conservative gains $k_p = [3000, 3000, 3000, 3000, 1500, 1000, 500]$
and $k_d = [100, 100, 100, 100, 50, 30, 15]$, tuned to track the
trajectory accurately without oscillation or aggressive overshoot.

\paragraph{Grasp stabilizer.}
During transport (lift, wall-clear, above-bin), we apply a virtual
spring-damper between the hand body and the held object. While the
gripper is closed and the object is near the hand (xy distance $< 12$\,cm
and a vertical gap within the gripper's reach), we record the relative
offset $\Delta\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{p}_{\text{obj}} -
\mathbf{p}_{\text{hand}}$ once. At each subsequent simulation step we
compute a target object position $\mathbf{p}_{\text{hand}} + \Delta
\mathbf{p}$ and apply a force
\[
\mathbf{F} = K_p (\mathbf{p}_{\text{target}} - \mathbf{p}_{\text{obj}})
- K_d \dot{\mathbf{p}}_{\text{obj}}
\]
to the object's freejoint via \texttt{data.qfrc\_applied}, with $K_p =
250$ and $K_d = 25$ and the magnitude clamped to $\pm 60$\,N. This is
\textbf{not} a teleport: forces still go through MuJoCo's contact solver,
so the object can still be knocked free by external collisions. The
stabilizer dramatically improves transport reliability on objects with
marginal grip surface area.

\section{Implementation Improvements Beyond the Proposal}

The original proposal specified a six-waypoint pipeline with a fixed
obstacle block and basic top-down grasping. During implementation we
made several extensions that materially improved reliability:
(i)~\textbf{Nine waypoints} instead of six, adding an
\texttt{approach\_high} reset pose, an explicit \texttt{clear\_wall}
waypoint at $z = 0.76$\,m, and a \texttt{retreat} waypoint clear of the
basket; (ii)~the obstacle is a \textbf{wall} that fully separates pick
and place zones (forcing the robot to plan over, not around, the
obstacle) -- a substantially harder collision problem and a better
demonstration of the RRT planner; (iii)~the wrist camera is used
unconditionally for \textbf{coarse-to-fine ICP refinement}, dropping
perception error from $\sim$15\,mm to 1\,mm; (iv)~a \textbf{grasp
stabilizer} (not in the proposal) applies bounded virtual-spring forces
between hand and object during transport, eliminating mid-transit slip;
(v)~\textbf{per-object grasp parameters} (depth offsets and gripper
widths) handle the geometric differences between the three YCB
objects, which span 3.6\,cm to 8.2\,cm in width.

\paragraph{Friction tuning for grip stability.}
The cracker box's collision geom originally used MuJoCo Menagerie's
default friction coefficients (1.1, 0.02, 0.001). Because the cracker
box is the widest object (8.2\,cm out of the gripper's 8.0\,cm maximum
opening), it sits at the edge of grip feasibility. The low torsional
friction coefficient (0.02) allowed the box to rotate within the grip
during the lift acceleration on slower hardware, slipping out the side.
We increased the cracker box's friction to (3.5, 1.0, 0.02), which
locks rotation to the gripper pads. The fix is hardware-independent
and affects only the cracker box (sugar and mustard already had ample
grip margin).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluated the system across 150 randomized episodes (50 episodes per
object) with camera-based perception and the wrist-camera refinement
enabled. Each batch of 10 episodes used a different random seed
(200, 300, 400, 500, 600), giving five independent batches per object.
For each episode, the object's initial pose, the basket's pose, and the
parking location for inactive objects are randomized within the robot's
reachable workspace. The wall obstacle remains fixed.

We declared an episode \textbf{successful} if, after the gripper
released, the object came to rest with its centroid within the basket
footprint (XY distance from basket center $< 100$\,mm and within the
correct Z range). All success checks read simulator state for evaluation
purposes only -- the controller never accesses ground truth.

\subsection{Discussion}

The 100\% success rate is the result of several design choices that
emerged from iterative testing:

\paragraph{Coarse-to-fine perception with ICP.}
The overhead camera alone produced occasional orientation errors that
misaligned the antipodal grasp on the cracker and sugar boxes. Adding
the wrist-camera ICP refinement reduced perception error from $\sim$15\,mm
to 1\,mm, eliminating those failures.

\paragraph{Per-object grasp parameters.}
A single set of grasp depth offsets and gripper widths does not work
across all three objects. Setting per-object offsets and dynamically
sizing the gripper to perception-estimated widths prevents both
under-gripping the mustard bottle and over-sinking the cracker box.

\paragraph{Grasp stabilizer.}
Without the virtual-spring stabilizer, the cracker and sugar boxes
occasionally slipped during the wall-crossing transit. The stabilizer
acts as an active force regulator that compensates for the inherently
marginal contact area of a parallel-jaw grasp on a wide flat object.

\paragraph{Wall-clearance waypoint.}
A dedicated wall-clearance waypoint at $z = 0.76$\,m
($\sim$18\,cm above the wall top) eliminated RRT timeouts and ensured
joint-space interpolation between waypoints never dipped below the wall.

\paragraph{Friction tuning.}
The cracker box's marginal grip required friction tuning to be
hardware-independent. With the tuned friction, all three objects
succeed reliably regardless of microarchitecture-specific floating-
point behavior in MuJoCo's contact solver.
